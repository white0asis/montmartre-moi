The hour Montmartre belongs only to the painters

Before the tour buses arrive, the square has a different rhythm entirely — here’s how to catch it.

C
Camille Roussel June 12, 2026 · 7 min read

There is an hour in Montmartre that the guidebooks never mention, because by the time most visitors arrive it has already passed. It begins a little before sunrise, when the funicular is still dark and the Place du Tertre belongs to no one — and then, slowly, to the painters.

They arrive on foot, carrying folding stools and boxes of pigment worn soft at the corners. There is no announcement, no opening bell. One easel goes up against the plane trees, then another, and within twenty minutes the square has quietly become a studio. This is the version of Montmartre that the neighborhood keeps for itself.

Why the early light matters

The painters are not here for the tourists — not yet. They are here because the light at this hour falls flat and even across the cobblestones, before the sun climbs high enough to throw the hard shadows that flatten a canvas. Ask any of them and they will tell you the same thing: the square is only worth painting twice a day, and the morning version is the one worth waking up for.

If you stand at the edge and watch, you will notice that almost none of them paint the Sacré-Cœur. The basilica is the postcard; they leave it to the afternoon crowd. Instead they turn their stools toward the corners — a shuttered café, a wedge of rooftop, the particular grey of a wall that has weathered a hundred winters.

“The square is only worth painting twice a day — and the morning version is the one worth waking up for.”

How to catch it

You do not need a plan, only an alarm. Aim to be on the square by 6:30am in summer, a little later in winter, when the painters drift in with the first grey light. Bring a coffee from one of the bakeries on the Rue des Abbesses that open before dawn — the terraces on the square itself will not stir for another two hours.

Stay to the edges, keep your voice down, and resist the urge to photograph faces. The painters are generous with the curious and impatient with the intrusive, and the difference between the two is mostly a matter of standing back. By nine the first tour groups crest the hill, the spell breaks, and Montmartre returns to everyone. But for those few hours just after dawn, it is the most private square in Paris.

Practical tip

The funicular doesn’t run this early, so come up the staircase from the Rue Foyatier — it’s steep, but the climb is part of the morning. A single Métro ticket covers the ride back down once it opens.
