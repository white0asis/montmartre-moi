Musée de Montmartre

The oldest surviving building in Montmartre, and the most quietly affecting museum in Paris. Renoir painted here. Suzanne Valadon lived here. The garden still looks the same.

About the museum

The Musée de Montmartre occupies the oldest house on the Butte — a 17th-century manor once used as a country retreat by a Parisian actor, later divided into studios rented out to artists who couldn't yet afford anything better. Renoir was here from 1875 to 1877, using the garden as the setting for some of his most famous paintings. Utrillo was here. Valadon was here, along with her son.

The permanent collection traces the artistic and social history of Montmartre from the mid-19th century to the interwar period — the rise of the cabarets, the bohemian communities, the posters, the politics. It does this without the triumphalism of larger institutions. Montmartre, the museum seems to understand, was always a little suspicious of being celebrated.

The recent renovations have added a Valadon wing and significantly expanded the garden. The café, which serves decent wine and reasonable food, overlooks the vineyard.
