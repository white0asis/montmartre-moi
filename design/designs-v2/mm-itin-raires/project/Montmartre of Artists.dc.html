A Day in the Montmartre of Artists

One unhurried loop through the Butte where modern art was actually made — from the studios of the Bateau-Lavoir to Renoir's windmill, ending in the garden above the last vineyard in Paris. Thirteen stops, built around the morning light and three good café stops.

🕐 ≈ 6 h 14 min 📍 13 stops 🚶 3.2 km Easy

What we love about this walk
🎨
It follows the painters, not the crowds The route traces where the art actually happened — Picasso's studio, Renoir's windmill, Valadon's house — mostly along the quiet back streets the tour groups never reach.
🌅
It's built around the light Start at Abbesses in the morning and you reach the vineyard and the best viewpoints just as the Butte begins to empty out for lunch. The timing is the whole trick.
☕
It knows when to stop Three café pauses are built into the loop — because a day in Montmartre that doesn't include sitting still for a while isn't really a day in Montmartre.

What you'll find on this walk

A preview of the route's highlights, in order. The full interactive itinerary lets you reorder, remove or add stops and open the whole thing in Google Maps.

1
Place des Abbesses
Square · 15 min · always open

The art-nouveau métro mouth by Guimard and the square where the day begins. The wall of "I love you" is a two-minute walk away.

2
Espace Dalí
Museum · 40 min · 5 min walk

The surrealist's sculptures and engravings, shown underground in a low, theatrical light. The one indoor stop of the morning.

3
Place du Tertre
Landmark · 25 min · 4 min walk

The painters' square, still crowded with easels. Touristy, yes — but it has been the working address of Montmartre's artists for a century and a half.

4
Musée de Montmartre MM Pick
Museum · 90 min · 3 min walk

The oldest house on the Butte, Renoir's garden, and Valadon's studio left exactly as she had it. The heart of the walk — read the full guide before you go.

5
Au Lapin Agile
Cabaret · 20 min · 6 min walk

The little pink-and-green cabaret Picasso once paid with a painting. Still hosts chansonniers most evenings, exactly as it did in 1900.

6
Le Bateau-Lavoir
Landmark · 15 min · 8 min walk

The ramshackle studio block where Picasso painted Les Demoiselles d'Avignon and Cubism was born. Only the shopfront window survives — the rest you have to imagine.

7
Moulin de la Galette
Landmark · 20 min · 5 min walk

The last working windmill on the hill, and the open-air dance garden Renoir turned into his most joyful canvas. A fine place to pause, glass in hand.

8
Clos Montmartre
Vineyard · 15 min · 4 min walk

The last working vineyard in Paris, hidden behind a wall on the north slope and harvested with a parade every October.

9
La Maison Rose
Café · 20 min · 3 min walk

Utrillo's little pink house, painted countless times and still serving lunch on the corner of Rue de l'Abreuvoir.

10
Rue de l'Abreuvoir
Street · 10 min · 2 min walk

The most painted street on the Butte — cobbles, ivy and the gentle bend that every postcard of Montmartre seems to borrow.

11
Allée des Brouillards
Hidden lane · 10 min · 5 min walk

A secret alley of gardens and shuttered houses where Renoir once lived, screened from the rest of the city by a curtain of trees.

12
Halle Saint-Pierre
Museum · 40 min · 9 min walk

At the foot of the hill, a museum of art brut and outsider art — a raw, living coda after a day spent among the old masters.

13
Le Mur des Je t'aime
Landmark · 10 min · 6 min walk

"I love you" in 311 languages on enamelled lava — a quiet full stop, back beside Abbesses where the loop began.

What you should know about the artists' Montmartre

A little context for the walk. Montmartre was the cradle of modern art for barely fifty years — and most of what made it possible has nothing to do with the paintings themselves.

🎨
Why the artists came

Cheap rents, clear north light and country air just outside the city's tax wall. Painters came for the economics as much as the romance — and stayed for both.

⏳
The golden years

Roughly 1870 to 1920 — from Renoir and the Impressionists to Picasso, Braque and the Cubists who upended painting in a draughty studio block.

🍷
It was a village

Windmills, vineyards and open-air dance gardens — half the appeal was that the Butte didn't really feel like Paris at all. Much of that village still shows through.

👁️
What's left today

The streets survive even where the studios don't. The Bateau-Lavoir is a window, the Lapin Agile a façade — so bring a little imagination along with the camera.

✊
More than painters

The Butte was radical as well as bohemian — the Commune began here in 1871, and that defiant streak ran through its cafés, its poets and its politics for decades after.

🎭
The cabaret side

Art and entertainment were never far apart here. The Chat Noir, the Lapin Agile and the Moulin Rouge all drew from — and fed — the same restless creative crowd.

Montmartre Moi tip

Do the loop anti-clockwise. You'll climb gently through the studios in the cool of the morning and come down the sunny south side toward lunch — instead of grinding up the stairs at midday with everyone else.
